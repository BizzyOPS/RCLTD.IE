ISO 13849 Updates: What You Need to Know

ISO 13849—the essential standard for safety-related parts of control systems—continues to evolve. Understanding recent updates and clarifications is critical for machinery manufacturers, system integrators, and anyone responsible for CE marking compliance in the European market.

Understanding ISO 13849: The Foundation

ISO 13849-1:2023 (the most recent edition) specifies safety requirements and guidance for the design and integration of safety-related parts of control systems (SRP/CS). It's the primary standard used for demonstrating compliance with the Machinery Directive 2006/42/EC and now the Machinery Regulation (EU) 2023/1230.

The standard applies to all types of machinery and all technologies—electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, and mechanical. Its risk-based approach determines required safety performance based on:

  • Severity of injury (S): S1 (slight) or S2 (serious)
  • Frequency of exposure (F): F1 (rare) or F2 (frequent)
  • Possibility of avoiding hazard (P): P1 (possible) or P2 (impossible)

These factors combine to determine the required Performance Level (PL)—from PLa (lowest) to PLe (highest).

Key Changes in the 2023 Edition

1. Clarified Software Requirements

The 2023 edition provides enhanced guidance on software development for safety systems. Key clarifications include:

  • More detailed requirements for software verification and validation
  • Clarified guidance on using pre-developed software libraries
  • Enhanced documentation requirements for software safety functions
  • Clearer requirements for software modification and version control

For integrators like ourselves, this means more rigorous documentation of PLC safety program development, including version control, testing protocols, and validation evidence.

2. Systematic Failure Considerations

The updated standard provides improved guidance on handling systematic failures—those resulting from errors in specification, design, or operation rather than random hardware failures. This includes:

  • Enhanced requirements for competency of design personnel
  • Clearer guidance on design review processes
  • Improved methods for demonstrating adequate design measures

3. Well-Tried Components

The concept of "well-tried components" has been clarified, particularly regarding how experience with components in non-safety applications can be leveraged for safety system design. The standard now provides:

  • Clearer criteria for what constitutes "well-tried"
  • Guidance on documentation requirements
  • Examples of acceptable evidence of field experience

4. Diagnostic Coverage (DC) Clarifications

The 2023 edition provides additional examples and guidance for calculating Diagnostic Coverage—a critical parameter in achieving required Performance Levels:

  • Enhanced tables of typical DC values for common components
  • Clarified methods for combining DC values in complex architectures
  • Improved guidance on test intervals and their impact on DC

Practical Application: The Performance Level Calculation

Achieving a required Performance Level involves careful component selection, architectural decisions, and verification calculations. Here's how it works in practice:

Step 1: Risk Assessment

Determine the required PL based on risk assessment of each safety function. For example, a robotic work cell where:

  • Severity = S2 (serious injury possible from robot collision)
  • Frequency = F2 (operators regularly enter cell)
  • Avoidance = P1 (with proper interlocking and lighting, avoidance is possible)

Result: Required PLr = PLd

Step 2: Architecture Selection

ISO 13849 defines several designated architectures:

  • Category B: Single channel with basic measures
  • Category 1: Single channel with well-tried components
  • Category 2: Single channel with monitoring
  • Category 3: Dual channel with single fault tolerance
  • Category 4: Dual channel with enhanced fault detection

For PLd, we typically use Category 3 architecture—dual channel with monitoring that detects single faults before the next demand.

Step 3: Component Selection and MTTF

Select components with appropriate Mean Time To Dangerous Failure (MTTFd) values:

  • Low: 3 years ≤ MTTFd < 10 years
  • Medium: 10 years ≤ MTTFd < 30 years
  • High: 30 years ≤ MTTFd ≤ 100 years

Safety PLCs and certified safety components typically provide MTTFd values in their documentation. For non-certified components, calculation methods in ISO 13849-1 and component failure rate data can be used.

Step 4: Diagnostic Coverage

Implement diagnostic measures and calculate DC:

  • None: DC < 60%
  • Low: 60% ≤ DC < 90%
  • Medium: 90% ≤ DC < 99%
  • High: DC ≥ 99%

Modern safety PLCs with cross-monitoring, discrepancy detection, and safety bus monitoring typically achieve Medium to High DC.

Step 5: Systematic Failure Avoidance

Demonstrate adequate measures against systematic failures through:

  • Qualified personnel (training, certification)
  • Structured development process
  • Design reviews and FMEA
  • Validation testing
  • Documentation of all design decisions

Step 6: Performance Level Verification

Combine Category, MTTFd, and DC to determine achieved PL using the performance level graph in ISO 13849-1. If achieved PL ≥ required PLr, the design is compliant.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Inadequate Risk Assessment

Many compliance issues stem from incomplete risk assessments. Every hazard must be identified, every safety function defined, and every PLr justified. Documentation is critical—vague risk assessments fail audits.

Using Non-Certified Components Incorrectly

While ISO 13849 allows non-certified components, they require rigorous evaluation. You must demonstrate adequate MTTFd, implement sufficient diagnostics, and document all assumptions. For critical functions, certified components simplify validation.

Ignoring Systematic Failures

Many designers focus exclusively on hardware failures and neglect systematic failure prevention. Software errors, wiring mistakes, and configuration errors are systematic failures that require different prevention strategies.

Insufficient Testing

ISO 13849 requires functional testing of safety systems. This includes:

  • Verification of all safety functions under normal conditions
  • Fault insertion testing to verify diagnostics detect single faults
  • Testing of override/bypass mechanisms
  • Verification of fail-safe behavior

Integration with Other Safety Standards

ISO 13849 doesn't exist in isolation. It must be applied alongside:

IEC 62061 (Electrical Safety Systems)

IEC 62061 offers an alternative approach using Safety Integrity Levels (SIL). While similar to ISO 13849's Performance Levels, IEC 62061:

  • Uses probability of dangerous failure per hour (PFHd) rather than PL
  • Focuses exclusively on electrical/electronic/programmable systems
  • Employs different calculation methods

Both standards are acceptable for demonstrating Machinery Directive compliance—the choice depends on system complexity and designer preference.

ISO 13855 (Positioning of Safeguards)

Determines minimum distances for positioning safeguards based on approach speeds and system stopping times.

ISO 12100 (Risk Assessment)

The overarching standard for machinery risk assessment—the foundation for determining required PLs.

Documentation Requirements

Compliance requires comprehensive documentation, including:

Safety Requirement Specification (SRS)

  • List of all safety functions
  • Required PL for each function
  • Operating modes and associated safety measures
  • Interfaces with other systems

Validation Documentation

  • Architecture descriptions and circuit diagrams
  • Component specifications and MTTFd values
  • DC calculations and diagnostic descriptions
  • PL calculation worksheets
  • Software documentation (for programmable systems)
  • Test plans and results

User Information

  • Operating instructions describing safety functions
  • Maintenance requirements and intervals
  • Guidance on modification and integration

The New Machinery Regulation

The Machinery Regulation (EU) 2023/1230, which will replace the Machinery Directive in January 2027, maintains ISO 13849 as a harmonized standard. Key changes affecting safety system design include:

  • Enhanced cybersecurity requirements for connected machinery
  • Clearer requirements for substantial modification
  • Improved guidance on AI and autonomous systems
  • Enhanced documentation requirements for digital systems

While these changes don't directly modify ISO 13849, they affect how we apply it—particularly regarding cybersecurity measures for safety systems.

Practical Tools and Resources

Several tools simplify ISO 13849 compliance:

SISTEMA Software

Free software from IFA (Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) for calculating Performance Levels. It handles complex architectures, provides component libraries, and generates documentation.

Safety PLCs with Integrated Calculation

Many safety PLC manufacturers (Siemens, Allen-Bradley, Pilz, etc.) provide software tools that calculate achieved PLs for their safety systems, simplifying validation.

Component Manufacturer Data

Safety component manufacturers typically provide detailed technical documentation including MTTFd values, DC calculations, and application guidance.

The Role of Competent Personnel

ISO 13849 explicitly requires that design, integration, and validation be performed by competent personnel. At Robotics & Control Ltd, our team includes:

  • Chartered engineers with machinery safety specialization
  • Certified functional safety engineers
  • Technicians with manufacturer-specific safety system training

This expertise ensures both compliance and optimal safety system design.

Conclusion

ISO 13849 continues to be the primary standard for machinery safety system design in Europe. The 2023 updates clarify requirements while maintaining the fundamental risk-based approach. Success requires:

  • Thorough risk assessment establishing required PLs
  • Appropriate architecture selection and component choices
  • Rigorous calculation and validation
  • Comprehensive documentation
  • Competent personnel and structured processes

Partner with experienced safety specialists who understand both the standard requirements and practical implementation challenges to ensure compliant, cost-effective safety solutions.
